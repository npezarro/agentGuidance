# Research & Reference Quality

When curating study resources, reference materials, competitive analysis, or any external content for the user, the quality bar is high. Generic content wastes the user's time and erodes trust.

## Principles

1. **Primary sources over commentary.** Company blogs, leadership interviews, official docs, and earnings transcripts beat third-party "guides" every time.
2. **Analysis from recognized voices.** Deconstructor of Fun, Naavik, Stratechery, Lenny's Newsletter, Matthew Ball — these have earned credibility. Random Medium posts and SEO-optimized listicles have not.
3. **Original data or original insight.** Every resource must contain at least one thing you can't get from reading the Wikipedia page. If a resource just restates publicly known facts with no added analysis, skip it.
4. **Verify before recommending.** WebFetch the URL and actually read the content. Don't recommend based on title or search snippet alone. If it's paywalled, say so explicitly and note what's visible in the preview.
5. **Fewer and better.** 15 excellent resources beats 75 mediocre ones. The user's time is the scarcest resource.

## Red Flags — Reject These

- "Ultimate Guide to X Interview" from SEO content farms (InterviewQuery, random .io sites)
- Listicles that restate a company's own documentation without adding insight
- "Top 10 Questions" posts with generic STAR-format answer templates
- Anything where the first 3 paragraphs are filler before getting to substance
- Resources that cite each other in a circle without any original source data
- Marketing content disguised as technical analysis (vendor blogs about their own products)

## What to Include

- Executive interviews with substantive strategy discussion (not PR puff)
- Industry analyst deep dives with original data or frameworks
- Technical blog posts from the company's own engineering team
- Academic or research-grade analysis of the market/technology
- Practitioner content from people who've done the specific work (not career coaches)
- Primary documents (culture memos, job postings read for signal, earnings transcripts)

## Format

When presenting resources, include for each:
- URL (verified accessible)
- Whether it's paywalled (and what's visible for free)
- 1-2 sentences on the specific unique insight it contains
- Why this resource over alternatives on the same topic

## Bulk Link Block

Any document with 3+ reference URLs must include an `## ALL LINKS` section at the very bottom with every URL on its own line — no bullets, no dashes, no numbering, no markdown. Just raw URLs linebroken. This allows the user to select-all and open them in bulk without copy-paste friction.

## Applies To

Any task involving: interview prep materials, competitive research, market analysis, technology deep dives, career/application materials, or any context where the user will invest time reading what you recommend.
